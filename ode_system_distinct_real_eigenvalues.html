Distinct Real Eigenvalues | Topic Playlist

The distinct real nonzero eigenvalues case is one of several solution types for a two dimensional system of linear, constant coefficient, homogeneous differential equations. The types of solutions are called stable nodes, unstable nodes, and saddles.

Solution | Explanation Video

When the system,

\begin{equation} \vec{x}' = A\vec{x} \qquad \lambda_{1} \neq \lambda_{2} \end{equation}

has distinct and real valued eigenvalues, the solution is,

\begin{equation} \vec{x}(t) = c_{1}e^{\lambda_{1}t}\vec{v}_{1} + c_{2}e^{\lambda_{2}t}\vec{v}_{2} \end{equation}

Where the eigenvectors are $\vec{v}_{1}$ and $\vec{v}_{2}$.

Phase Portraits | Explanation Video

The sign of the eigenvalues determines the type of phase portrait.

For $\lambda_{1} < \lambda_{2} < 0$, the solution type is a stable node.

The red diagonal trajectory is the $\vec{v}_{1}$ eigendirection because $\lambda_{1}$ is the most negative eigenvalue, causing that eigendirection to decay fastest.

For $0 < \lambda_{1} < \lambda_{2}$, the solution type is an unstable node.

The red diagonal trajectory is the $\vec{v}_{2}$ eigendirection because $\lambda_{2}$ is the most positive eigenvalue, causing that eigendirection to grow the fastest.

For $\lambda_{1} < 0 < \lambda_{2}$, the solution type is an saddle.

The red diagonal trajectory is the $\vec{v}_{1}$ eigendirection because $\lambda_{1} < 0$ and that eigendirection is decaying. The red horizontal trajectory is the $\vec{v}_{2}$ eigendirection because $0 < \lambda_{2}$ and that eigendirection is growing.

The case when at least one of $\lambda_{1}$ or $\lambda_{2}$ is zero are either constant or non-isolated fixed points solutions. These are borderline cases and are handled on a separate page.

Example 1 | Solution Video

Solve the following IVP, then draw a phase portrait with the eigendirections and the solution trajectory forward and backward in time.

\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} x' \\ y' \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 0 & -4 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} \quad \begin{bmatrix} x(0) \\ y(0) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
