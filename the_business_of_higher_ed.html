Higher Education is a business. And like a business, Higher Education is trying to sell you something. As the old adage goes, "You can never trust a person that needs something from you." It's in a university's best interest to convince prospective students that paying their tuition is a good investment. But not just the university, individual majors are also fighting for a limited number of enrolled students. While they don't outright lie due to the consequences, there's a lot of misleading and omitted information in their promotions.

Even so, there are some things prospective students can look out for when picking a university or major or graduate advisor. That includes if you'll like the jobs your degree is applicable to. For instance, the first job of many STEM graduates is not in STEM. There are multiple reasons for this, but one reason is STEM graduates didn't really like what they had studied, and the reality of doing what you studied forever in a STEM career is unappealing, so they got a non-STEM job after graduation.

Picking a Grad Advisor | Video

Not all advisors are equal. Some are better at mentoring than others. Some are not well connected when you graduate. Some care about your wellbeing, while other do not.

Knowing the path from undergrad to tenured professor helps to understand why grad advisors can be exploitive.

At the end of a PhD program, the doctor has learned how to complete and publish novel research. Next is the postdoc, where a PhD graduate typically goes to another university and continues to publish for two to three years. Your quantity and quality of publications during your postdoc determines which universities you will be accepted for a tenure track position. The tenure process lasts around six years, with some updates to let you know if you're on track to get tenure. If you fail to get tenure, the options are start over at another university or leave academia.

There are a few types of grad advisors. One is in the process of getting tenure, another prioritizes graduating students, and another cares about research.

Curved Grading | Video

Curved grading is actually very beneficial for lecturers by obscuring their teaching ability.

curved course sequence bad professor and great professor for first course in sequence second course average professor expect a split bell curve

For simplicity, lets look at a first course in a sequence, like calculus or chemistry or physics, in the fall term that has 250 students in the course. The expectation is the backgrounds and capabilities of this group of 250 students should be random and as a result, the grade distribution will be a bell curve.

Did you know that some universities used to have shared final exams for courses with multiple sections, such three separate courses of Calculus I offered in the same term with three different lecturers?

Here's an exam which has a bimodal grade distribution, which is fancy for two spikes.

This wasn't from half the students being competent and the rest being incompetent. It turned out that the lecturer had assumed all the students learned something from a previous course because the lecturers decided to teach different things.

The lecturer decided to not count the exam in the grade, but this could have been prevented with more standardization of the prerequisite course.
